Catalysis

It is the phenomenon of increasing the speed of a chemical reaction through the use of a catalyst, a substance that is neither a raw material nor a product of that transformation. Accelerating a reaction, favoring the production of certain products, minimizing the formation of by-products, and/or reducing the energy requirements of a chemical process are possible catalytic effects. Therefore, catalysis contributes to reducing the environmental impact of processes and promoting economic sustainability.

Catalytic lines of interest:

  • Development of catalytic processes for the transformation of resources, both conventional, non-conventional, and renewable, into high-value-added chemical compounds and/or for energy use.
  • Valorization of waste and/or by-products from agro-industries, seeking to apply the concept of biorefinery.
    • Example: catalytic conversion of glycerol, a by-product of biodiesel, through the following processes:
      • Selective reduction to propylene glycol (PG);
      • Hydrogenolysis to ethylene glycol (EG) and methanol;
      • Gas-phase reforming to hydrogen (H2) and/or synthesis gas;
      • Selective catalytic oxidation to lactic acid; and
      • Direct carbonylation to glycerol carbonate.
    • Utilization of biomass to obtain high-value-added products used as supports for catalysts.
